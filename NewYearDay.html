The celebration of the “New Year” is primarily linked to the Western calendar and the tradition of marking time based on the birth of Jesus. The New Year begins on January 1st, commemorating the Feast of the Solemnity of Mary, Mother of God. This date was established by the Gregorian calendar, introduced by Pope Gregory XIII in 1582 to reform the earlier Julian calendar. On the other hand, Hinduism follows a lunar calendar, and the celebration of the New Year varies across different regions and traditions within Hinduism. The most widely celebrated Hindu New Year is known as "Vikram Samvat”. The Vikram Samvat calendar is believed to have been initiated by the legendary king Vikramaditya and is based on the lunar calendar. The Hindu New Year typically falls in the spring months, with different regions celebrating it at various times. While the Christian and Hindu New Years are distinct in terms of their religious and cultural contexts, it's interesting to note that both traditions share a common theme of renewal and the passage of time. The concept of marking the beginning of a new year with festivities, reflection, and resolutions is a universal human practice found in various cultures around the world, each with its unique historical and religious roots.
